## üîπ Helping (Auxiliary) Verbs

## üîπ Special Cases

### ‚úî Two Singular Subjects

### ‚úî Indefinite Pronouns  
Words like *everyone, somebody, nobody* use **singular verbs**:
- *Somebody **left** the door open.*  
- *Nobody **knows** the answer.*

The word ‚Äúleft‚Äù in ‚ÄúSomebody left the door open‚Äù is the past tense of the verb ‚Äúleave.‚Äù And in the past tense, verbs don‚Äôt get an -s at the end, no matter if the subject is singular or plural.
Present tense (needs the -s for singular subjects):

Past tense (no -s for anyone):

He left early.

Somebody left the window open.

They left early.

## üìù Lesson 5: Phrasal Verbs & Collocations

In this section, we dive into **phrasal verbs** and **collocations**, which are essential for sounding more like a native English speaker. These aspects of English will help you understand how words are commonly paired together in everyday language.

### üîπ **Phrasal Verbs**
Phrasal verbs are expressions that combine verbs with particles (adverbs or prepositions). They have meanings that may not be obvious from the individual words. Mastering phrasal verbs can make your speech sound more fluent and natural.

#### Examples of Phrasal Verbs:
- **Work out** ‚Äì I usually work out 2 or 3 times a week.
- **Wake up** ‚Äì What time do you wake up on the weekend?
- **Turn down** ‚Äì Turn down the TV, it‚Äôs too loud!
- **Throw away** ‚Äì Throw away any old cardboard in the recycling bin.
- **Look for** ‚Äì I‚Äôm looking for my phone, I lost it last night.

### üîπ **Transitive vs. Intransitive Phrasal Verbs**
- **Transitive Phrasal Verbs**: These require an object after the verb.
  - Example: ‚ÄúI‚Äôve always looked up to Bill Gates.‚Äù
  
- **Intransitive Phrasal Verbs**: These cannot have an object; they work alone.
  - Example: ‚ÄúShe‚Äôs really grown up.‚Äù

### üîπ **Separable vs. Inseparable Phrasal Verbs**
- **Separable Phrasal Verbs**: These can have words between the verb and particle.
  - Example: ‚ÄúGive my phone back to me.‚Äù
  
- **Inseparable Phrasal Verbs**: These cannot have words inserted between the verb and particle.
  - Example: ‚ÄúPlease look after my pet goldfish.‚Äù

### üîπ **Common Phrasal Verbs**
Here are some more common phrasal verbs:
- **Put on**: Why don‚Äôt you put on your favorite shirt today?
- **Pass out**: We ran so far we nearly passed out!
- **Hold on**: Hold on, I‚Äôll be with you in just a minute.
- **Give up**: I should really give up smoking this year.

---

### üîπ **Collocations**
Collocations are combinations of words that are commonly used together. Understanding collocations can help you speak more naturally and sound fluent.

#### Examples of Collocations:
- **Adverb + Adjective**:  
  ‚ÄúI won‚Äôt leave a review until I‚Äôm **completely satisfied**.‚Äù
  
- **Adjective + Noun**:  
  ‚ÄúI feel **ridiculously tired** this morning.‚Äù

- **Noun + Noun**:  
  ‚ÄúMy little brother is getting really **cheeky**.‚Äù

- **Verb + Expression with Preposition**:  
  ‚ÄúI **come from** a very big family.‚Äù

- **Verb + Noun**:  
  ‚ÄúWe watched an **action movie** at the cinema.‚Äù

- **Verb + Adverb**:  
  ‚ÄúHe‚Äôs been reading **quietly** for an hour now.‚Äù

---

### üîπ **Using Phrasal Verbs and Collocations**
- **Be mindful of context**: Phrasal verbs and collocations often depend on the situation. Make sure you know when and where to use them.
- **Don‚Äôt overuse them**: While phrasal verbs and collocations are important for fluency, using them excessively may sound forced. Focus on using them naturally.

> **Tip**: Try using a phrasal verb or collocation in a sentence today to practice its use!

## üï∞Ô∏è **1. Simple Tenses**

### **Past Simple**
- **Usage**: Actions that are **completed** in the past.
- **Structure**: Subject + Verb (past)  
  *Example*: "I listened to music."
- **Negative**: Subject + didn‚Äôt + Verb (base)  
  *Example*: "She didn‚Äôt call."

### **Present Simple**
- **Usage**: General facts, habitual actions, or actions happening now.
- **Structure**: Subject + Verb (present)  
  *Example*: "I play soccer."
- **Negative**: Subject + don‚Äôt/doesn‚Äôt + Verb (base)  
  *Example*: "He doesn‚Äôt like coffee."
  
### **Future Simple**
- **Usage**: Actions that **haven‚Äôt happened yet**.
- **Structure**: Subject + **will** + Verb (base)  
  *Example*: "I will eat breakfast tomorrow."
- **Negative**: Subject + **won‚Äôt** + Verb (base)  
  *Example*: "They won‚Äôt come to the party."

---

## üîÑ **2. Continuous Tenses**

### **Past Continuous**
- **Usage**: Ongoing actions in the **past**.
- **Structure**: Subject + was/were + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "I was eating when she arrived."

### **Present Continuous**
- **Usage**: Actions happening **now** or around the present.
- **Structure**: Subject + am/are/is + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "I am studying."
- **Negative**: Subject + am/are/is + not + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "She isn‚Äôt working today."

### **Future Continuous**
- **Usage**: Ongoing actions in the **future**.
- **Structure**: Subject + **will** + be + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "We‚Äôll be waiting at 5 PM."

---

## ‚úÖ **3. Perfect Tenses**

### **Past Perfect**
- **Usage**: Actions that **happened before** another past event.
- **Structure**: Subject + had + Verb (Past Participle)  
  *Example*: "I had finished before you arrived."

### **Present Perfect**
- **Usage**: Past actions with a **current** effect.
- **Structure**: Subject + has/have + Verb (Past Participle)  
  *Example*: "I have eaten."
- **Negative**: Subject + hasn‚Äôt/haven‚Äôt + Verb (Past Participle)  
  *Example*: "He hasn‚Äôt visited."

### **Future Perfect**
- **Usage**: Actions that will be **completed** before another future event.
- **Structure**: Subject + **will** + have + Verb (Past Participle)  
  *Example*: "She will have left by the time we arrive."
- **Negative**: Subject + **won‚Äôt** + have + Verb (Past Participle)  
  *Example*: "We won‚Äôt have finished by 5 PM."

---

## üîÑ ‚úÖ **4. Perfect Continuous Tenses**

### **Past Perfect Continuous**
- **Usage**: Ongoing actions in the past, continuing until another event.
- **Structure**: Subject + had + been + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "She had been running before the race started."

### **Present Perfect Continuous**
- **Usage**: Actions that started in the past and **continue** into the present.
- **Structure**: Subject + has/have + been + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "I have been studying for hours."
- **Negative**: Subject + hasn‚Äôt/haven‚Äôt + been + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "She hasn‚Äôt been reading."

### **Future Perfect Continuous**
- **Usage**: Ongoing actions in the future that continue until another event.
- **Structure**: Subject + **will** + have + been + Verb(ing)  
  *Example*: "We will have been working for 5 hours by 5 PM."

---

## **Best Practices for Using Tenses**

1. **Consistency**: Stick to one tense when talking about a specific time period. Switching tenses can confuse your audience.
2. **Context**: Always consider the **time** of the action. Use the appropriate tense for past, present, or future.
3. **Clarity**: Use the continuous forms for actions happening **over time** and perfect tenses for actions that are **completed** or have an impact on the present or future.
4. **Time Expressions**: Use time expressions like **yesterday**, **right now**, **tomorrow**, **since**, and **by the time** to clearly signal when an action occurs.
5. **Practice**: Read a lot of material in English and write frequently to get a feel for when each tense is used.

## 1. **Understanding Sentences**

A **sentence** is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. It usually contains a subject and a verb and can make a statement, ask a question, or give an order.

* **Subject**: The person or thing performing the action.
* **Predicate**: The part of the sentence that tells what the subject does.

### Example:

* "The young boy waited in line at the shop."

### Sentence Structure:

The subject is the **who** or **what** that is doing the action, and the predicate is the **action** or state being described.

---

## 2. **Basic Sentence Types**

There are four main types of sentences in English. Each type serves a different purpose and ends with a different punctuation mark.

### **1. Declarative Sentences**

These sentences provide information or state facts. They end with a **period** (".").

* **Example**: "The weather is sunny today."

### **2. Interrogative Sentences**

These sentences ask questions and end with a **question mark** ("?").

* **Example**: "What time is the meeting?"

### **3. Exclamatory Sentences**

Used to show strong feelings or surprise, these sentences end with an **exclamation mark** ("!").

* **Example**: "Wow, that‚Äôs amazing!"

### **4. Imperative Sentences**

These give commands, suggestions, or requests. They may end with either an **exclamation mark** or a **period**.

* **Example**: "Please pass me the salt."
* **Example**: "Stop right there!"

---

## 3. **Mastering Nouns and Pronouns**

### **Nouns**

A **noun** is a word that represents a person, place, thing, or idea. Nouns often serve as the subject of the sentence.

#### Types of Nouns:

* **Common Nouns**: General names like "dog," "city," or "car."
* **Proper Nouns**: Specific names, always capitalized, such as "John," "Paris," or "The Mona Lisa."
* **Abstract Nouns**: Ideas or concepts like "freedom," "happiness," or "love."
* **Concrete Nouns**: Things we can see or touch, like "book," "apple," or "computer."
* **Countable Nouns**: Things you can count, such as "apple" or "child."
* **Uncountable Nouns**: Things that cannot be counted individually, like "water," "rice," or "advice."

---

### **Pronouns**

A **pronoun** replaces a noun to avoid repetition. There are several types of pronouns:

#### Types of Pronouns:

* **Personal Pronouns**: Replace specific nouns. Examples: "he," "she," "it," "we," "they."
* **Reflexive Pronouns**: Indicate the subject and object are the same, ending in ‚Äú-self‚Äù or ‚Äú-selves.‚Äù Examples: "myself," "yourself," "themselves."
* **Demonstrative Pronouns**: Point to specific things. Examples: "this," "that," "these," "those."
* **Interrogative Pronouns**: Used to ask questions. Examples: "who," "what," "where."
* **Indefinite Pronouns**: Refer to non-specific things or people. Examples: "everyone," "somebody," "anything."

---

## 4. **Possessive Forms**

Possession indicates ownership and can be shown with **apostrophes** in writing.

* **Singular Possession**: "David‚Äôs pen"
* **Plural Possession**: "The shops‚Äô doors"
* **Possessive Pronouns**: Replace the possessive adjective + noun (e.g., "This is my book" ‚Üí "This book is mine").

---

## 5. **Demonstratives and Possession**

### **Demonstratives**

Demonstratives are words used to point to specific things or show emphasis.

* **This**: Refers to something close.
* **That**: Refers to something farther away.
* **These**: Refers to multiple things close.
* **Those**: Refers to multiple things farther away.

#### Examples:

* **This book is amazing.**
* **I can see those cars.**

### **Possessive Pronouns**

Possessive pronouns show ownership. They replace a possessive adjective and a noun.

* **Mine** (for "my")
* **Yours** (for "your")
* **His** (for "his")
* **Hers** (for "her")
* **Ours** (for "our")
* **Theirs** (for "their")
